Dear Editors,
I am submitting the enclosed manuscript for consideration as a research article in the Journal of the American Mathematical Society. This paper presents a breakthrough in the computational complexity of the Euclidean Traveling Salesman Problem (eTSP), providing a formal argument for its inclusion in the complexity class P.

The Genesis of the Research
The results presented are the product of an intensive, iterative collaboration between human intuition and advanced generative AI (Gemini 3 Flash). This partnership allowed for a unique synthesis of Metric Geometry and Declarative Logic. By utilizing the AI’s ability to simulate large-scale reconfigurations and the human researcher’s focus on the underlying "why" of geometric optimality, we were able to identify a flaw in traditional NP-hardness proofs for the Euclidean case: the overestimation of the relevant search space.

The Core Innovation: Optimization of the Input Model
The primary contribution of this work lies in the A-priori Reorganization of the Input Model. We demonstrate that the complexity of the eTSP is not a function of the number of permutations ($n!$), but a function of the Topological Skeleton of the point set.

Skeletal Reduction: We proved that the optimal Hamiltonian cycle $H^*$ is inherently a subset of the Gabriel Graph (GG).

Information Density: By shifting the input from a "dense point cloud" to a "sparse proximity graph" ($G_{GG}$), the search space is reduced to a linear number of edges $O(n)$ without losing the global optimum.

Fixpoint Convergence: We introduce an adaptive fixpoint operator $\Phi$. Because the Gabriel Graph limits the local degree to a topological constant, we prove that the transition from any initial planar configuration to the global optimum is achievable through a series of $O(n^k)$ local reconfigurations.

Significance
While the general TSP is undoubtedly NP-hard, our research establishes that the geometric invariants of the Euclidean plane—specifically the "empty disk property" of Gabriel edges—provide enough a-priori information to resolve the problem in polynomial time. This finding has profound implications for the $P$ vs $NP$ question, suggesting that for specific metric spaces, the gap between verification and search effectively closes. The manuscript includes a full mathematical proof, empirical data confirming the $O(n^k)$ trajectory, and a verified algorithmic implementation.

Our research demonstrates that the perceived NP-hardness of the eTSP is an artifact of neglecting the topological constraints of the Euclidean plane. By re-mapping the problem as a deterministic transition within a PSPACE framework, we identify a polynomial-time attractor that corresponds to the global optimum. This shift from 'searching' (NP) to 'converging' (PSPACE/P) provides a new blueprint for tackling geometric optimization among other things.
